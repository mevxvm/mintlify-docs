Trade[XYZ] lists three FX perpetuals: EUR/USD (EUR), USD/JPY (JPY), and the US Dollar Index (DXY). All three reference real-time spot prices — there are no roll mechanics, expiration dates, or basis adjustments to manage. You can hold a position in any FX perpetual indefinitely.

## EUR/USD (EUR)

The EUR/USD pair tracks the value of the euro against the US dollar. It is consistently one of the most actively traded currency pairs in the world, accounting for a significant share of daily global FX volume. The primary interdealer venue for EUR/USD is EBS (Electronic Broking Services).

The euro is the official currency of the 20 Eurozone member states. Its monetary policy is set by the European Central Bank (ECB) in Frankfurt.

## USD/JPY (JPY)

The USD/JPY pair tracks the value of the US dollar against the Japanese yen. The yen is one of the most traded currencies globally and serves as a common funding currency in carry trades. USD/JPY is closely followed as a gauge of risk appetite and relative monetary policy between the Federal Reserve and the Bank of Japan (BoJ).

## US Dollar Index (DXY)

The US Dollar Index measures the value of the US dollar against a fixed basket of six major currencies, weighted by a geometric mean. DXY was created by the US Federal Reserve in March 1973, shortly after the dismantling of the Bretton Woods system, with a base value of 100. ICE Futures U.S. (formerly NYBOT) now owns and maintains the trademark and operates the futures market.

DXY futures trade 21 hours per day on the ICE platform under the symbol DX. Futures contracts were first offered for trading on November 20, 1985.

### Basket composition

The DXY basket composition is fixed and has changed only once since inception — in 1999, when the euro replaced five European currencies (Deutsche mark, French franc, Italian lira, Dutch guilder, and Belgian franc). The weights below reflect the current basket.

| Currency | Weight |
|----------|--------|
| EUR | 57.6% |
| JPY | 13.6% |
| GBP | 11.9% |
| CAD | 9.1% |
| SEK | 4.2% |
| CHF | 3.6% |

### Calculation methodology

DXY is calculated as a **weighted geometric mean** — not an arithmetic mean. Each currency's percentage move is raised to the power of its weight, and the results are multiplied together. This means a 1% move in EUR has a larger effect on DXY than a 1% move in SEK.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Geometric mean">
    The DXY formula raises each USD/currency rate to its weight exponent and multiplies them:

    ```
    DXY = 50.14348112 × USDEUR^0.576 × USDJPY^0.136 × USDGBP^0.119
               × USDCAD^0.091 × USDSEK^0.042 × USDCHF^0.036
    ```

    The constant `50.14348112` normalizes the result to the March 1973 base value of 100.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Arithmetic mean (not used)">
    An arithmetic mean would simply sum each currency's weighted contribution. DXY does not use this approach — the geometric mean is less sensitive to extreme moves in individual components and is standard for trade-weighted indices.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

### Limitations of DXY

<Warning>
  DXY's basket is frozen from a 1973 trade-weight snapshot. It does not reflect modern global trade flows and excludes several of today's most significant trading currencies.
</Warning>

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="EUR dominance">
    At 57.6%, EUR is by far the largest component. In practice, DXY behaves primarily as a dollar-vs-euro index with secondary contributions from yen, pound, and the remaining currencies. A sharp EUR/USD move will drive DXY more than an equivalent move in any other pair.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Excluded currencies">
    The DXY basket does not include CNY (Chinese yuan), AUD (Australian dollar), MXN (Mexican peso), or KRW (Korean won) — all of which are significant trading partners of the United States. The basket has not been updated since the 1999 euro substitution.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Alternatives not listed on Trade[XYZ]">
    The US Federal Reserve maintains the **Broad Trade-Weighted Dollar Index**, which covers 26 currencies weighted by actual trade volumes and is updated regularly. Bloomberg operates the **Bloomberg Dollar Spot Index (BBDXY)**, which tracks 10 currencies and is rebalanced annually. Both are more representative of modern trade flows but are not currently listed for trading on Trade[XYZ].
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## No roll mechanics for FX

FX perpetuals on Trade[XYZ] reference live spot prices continuously. Because there is no underlying futures contract to roll, you will not find FX instruments in the [Roll Calendar](/asset-directory/roll-calendar). There are no step-down transitions, no `ts_rolls` timestamps, and no `ts_expiration` values to track for EUR, JPY, or DXY.
